Demographic Composition of the Clean Energy Workforce Link to heading

Visual 1: Race/Ethnicity Distribution by Technology (Stacked Bar with Labels) Link to heading

This analysis presents the baseline demographic composition of the clean energy workforce across selected technologies. A stacked bar chart was constructed to display the proportional representation of major racial and ethnic groups within key technologies, including Solar, Wind, Nuclear, Natural Gas, Hydropower (low-impact and traditional), and Energy Efficiency. The results reveal clear disparities across technologies:

  • White workers consistently form the largest share, particularly in Nuclear and Natural Gas sectors.
  • Hispanic or Latino workers are notably concentrated in Solar and Energy Efficiency, suggesting industry-specific clustering.
  • Black or African American workers are underrepresented across most technologies, with relatively higher shares only in select subsectors.
  • Asian workers have a stronger presence in high-skill, capital-intensive sectors such as Nuclear.
  • Representation of Indigenous, Pacific Islander, and multi-racial groups remains limited, with each generally below 5%. To ensure readability, labels were placed directly within each stacked segment for groups exceeding 3% share, providing both visual clarity and precise numeric context. This visualization serves as a baseline benchmark for understanding racial and ethnic representation in the clean energy workforce. It highlights both areas of diversity and persistent gaps, which form the foundation for subsequent analyses of gender differences, wage disparities, and structural inequities in later sections.

Visual 2: Clean Energy Workforce by Gender (Share %) Link to heading

This figure presents the gender distribution across the same set of technologies used in the race/ethnicity baseline (Solar, Wind, Nuclear EPG, Natural Gas EPG, Low-impact Hydropower, Traditional Hydropower, and Energy Efficiency). We subset the USEER workforce-characteristics tables to the categories Male, Female, and Gender non-binary, then computed each technology‚Äôs average share (%) by gender. To ensure comparability with the prior chart, technologies were aligned to the pivot_race index. Bars are labeled with percentage values (one decimal place) to facilitate like-for-like comparisons across technologies.

  • The height of each bar indicates the average share of that gender within the technology‚Äôs workforce.
  • On-bar labels (e.g., ‚Äúxx.x%‚Äù) provide exact values, reducing ambiguity where bars are visually close. Because we use shares, cross-technology differences reflect composition, not headcount size.
  • Key findings
    • Male workers constitute the majority across all technologies, with the highest male shares typically observed in construction- and operations-intensive segments (e.g., Wind, Nuclear EPG, Natural Gas EPG).
    • Female representation is relatively higher in Energy Efficiency and Solar, though still below parity in most cases.
    • Gender non-binary shares appear small but non-zero in several technologies; given low prevalence, these estimates should be interpreted cautiously (potential sample and rounding effects).
  • Interpretation & context
    • The pattern aligns with occupational segregation observed in energy and construction value chains‚Äîinstallation, O&M, and field roles skew male, while segments with larger proportions of project management, sales, and outreach show slightly higher female shares.
    • These compositional differences provide important context for wage-gap analysis in ¬ß3.3‚Äì3.5: if female workers are under-represented in the highest-paying technologies or roles, aggregate pay gaps may reflect both price effects (different pay for similar roles) and mix effects (different roles/technologies).

Visual 3: BIPOC Representation Gap vs. National Workforce Average Link to heading

Representation Gap (BIPOC) by Technology, percentage points (pp). This figure quantifies how BIPOC (all non-White groups combined) are represented in each clean-energy technology relative to the U.S. national workforce average. Positive values indicate over-representation (BIPOC share in that technology exceeds national average); negative values indicate under-representation.

  • Method

    • BIPOC share by technology: From the USEER workforce-characteristics tables, we aggregate all non-White categories (Hispanic/Latino, Black or African American (non-Indigenous), Asian, American Indian/Alaska Native, Native Hawaiian/Pacific Islander, Two or more races). For each technology and survey sheet, we compute the mean share (%).
    • Baseline: From the same tables‚Äô single-column baselines, we compute National Workforce Averages for the same race/ethnicity categories and aggregate to national BIPOC share.

  • Gap (pp): Representation Gap (pp) = BIPOCshare in technology - BIPOCshare in national workforce We align technologies to those in the prior visuals (Solar, Wind, Nuclear EPG, Natural Gas EPG, Low-impact Hydropower, Traditional Hydropower, Energy Efficiency) to ensure comparability. Bars are labeled with ¬±pp values, and the 0 line marks parity with the national workforce.

  • Key findings

    • Solar and Energy Efficiency typically exhibit smaller negative gaps or modest positive gaps, indicating BIPOC participation is closer to (or slightly above) national averages in these entry-growth segments.
    • Nuclear EPG and Natural Gas EPG generally show larger negative gaps, signaling under-representation of BIPOC workers in higher-paying, capital-intensive technologies.
    • Hydropower (traditional and low-impact) tends to sit near or modestly below parity, but patterns can vary by sheet/aggregation.

  • Interpretation & implications

    • The pattern suggests pipeline segmentation: BIPOC workers are more present in installation/efficiency/front-of-meter growth roles (e.g., Solar, EE), but are under-represented in legacy, higher-wage, or licensing-intensive technologies (e.g., Nuclear, Natural Gas).
    • Where the gap is negative and large, the sector likely faces barriers to entry and advancement (credentialing, prior experience screens, union access pathways, or geographic clustering). These are priority areas for equity interventions (e.g., apprenticeships, credential bridging, targeted recruitment, and sponsorship/mentorship).
