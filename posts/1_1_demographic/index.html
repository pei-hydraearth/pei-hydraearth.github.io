Demographic Composition of the Clean Energy Workforce Link to heading

Abstract Link to heading

This study focuses on diversity and workforce equity in the U.S. clean energy sector, combining USEER 2024 raw data with external sources (ACS PUMS, BLS, McKinsey, etc.) to examine who works in the clean energy industry, whether they receive fair compensation, and the impact of diversity on industry performance. Our analysis reveals: Representation: There are significant disparities in the racial and gender composition of the clean energy sector, with certain groups (such as women, Latinos, and Blacks) still significantly underrepresented in emerging fields like Solar and Wind compared to the national average. Equity & Pay Gap: Preliminary proxy analysis indicates that gender and racial pay gaps persist and even widen in clean energy employment, with some technical fields showing more pronounced disparities. Performance Impact: Diversity is not only a fairness issue but also a key factor in ROI and competitiveness. Research and case studies indicate that increasing diversity can significantly improve company profitability, retention rates, and ESG scores. These findings not only provide policymakers with data-driven decision-making insights but also serve as a narrative and business value proposition for companies and investors to advance workforce equity.

Introduction Link to heading

As the U.S. energy transition accelerates, the clean energy sector is expanding rapidly. However, in this emerging job market, issues of diversity and wage equity remain unresolved. Women and minorities have historically been underrepresented in STEM and energy-related occupations, and wage gaps are widespread. This study aims to systematically analyze the current state of diversity and equity in clean energy employment and address three core questions:

Who is in clean energy jobs? Link to heading

What are the participation rates of different ethnic groups, genders, and age groups in sectors such as solar, wind, efficiency, and vehicles? Are there representation gaps compared to national averages?

Are they paid equally? Link to heading

After controlling for factors such as education and industry, do significant gender and racial wage gaps still exist? Which industries or technologies exhibit the most pronounced gaps?

Why does it matter? Link to heading

How does diversity impact productivity, profitability, employee retention, and ESG scores? Can a clear return on investment (ROI) narrative be established? Using USEER raw data + ACS PUMS wage data + external research evidence, this study not only outlines the diversity landscape of clean energy employment but also provides actionable recommendations and business value propositions to support policy advancement and corporate decision-making.

Visual 1: Race/Ethnicity Distribution by Technology (Stacked Bar with Labels) Link to heading

This analysis presents the baseline demographic composition of the clean energy workforce across selected technologies. A stacked bar chart was constructed to display the proportional representation of major racial and ethnic groups within key technologies, including Solar, Wind, Nuclear, Natural Gas, Hydropower (low-impact and traditional), and Energy Efficiency. The results reveal clear disparities across technologies:

  • White workers consistently form the largest share, particularly in Nuclear and Natural Gas sectors.
  • Hispanic or Latino workers are notably concentrated in Solar and Energy Efficiency, suggesting industry-specific clustering.
  • Black or African American workers are underrepresented across most technologies, with relatively higher shares only in select subsectors.
  • Asian workers have a stronger presence in high-skill, capital-intensive sectors such as Nuclear.
  • Representation of Indigenous, Pacific Islander, and multi-racial groups remains limited, with each generally below 5%. To ensure readability, labels were placed directly within each stacked segment for groups exceeding 3% share, providing both visual clarity and precise numeric context. This visualization serves as a baseline benchmark for understanding racial and ethnic representation in the clean energy workforce. It highlights both areas of diversity and persistent gaps, which form the foundation for subsequent analyses of gender differences, wage disparities, and structural inequities in later sections.

Visual 3: BIPOC Representation Gap vs. National Workforce Average Link to heading

Representation Gap (BIPOC) by Technology, percentage points (pp). This figure quantifies how BIPOC (all non-White groups combined) are represented in each clean-energy technology relative to the U.S. national workforce average. Positive values indicate over-representation (BIPOC share in that technology exceeds national average); negative values indicate under-representation.

  • Method BIPOC share by technology: From the USEER workforce-characteristics tables, we aggregate all non-White categories (Hispanic/Latino, Black or African American (non-Indigenous), Asian, American Indian/Alaska Native, Native Hawaiian/Pacific Islander, Two or more races). For each technology and survey sheet, we compute the mean share (%). Baseline: From the same tables‚Äô single-column baselines, we compute National Workforce Averages for the same race/ethnicity categories and aggregate to national BIPOC share.
  • Key findings
    • Solar and Energy Efficiency typically exhibit smaller negative gaps or modest positive gaps, indicating BIPOC participation is closer to (or slightly above) national averages in these entry-growth segments.
    • Nuclear EPG and Natural Gas EPG generally show larger negative gaps, signaling under-representation of BIPOC workers in higher-paying, capital-intensive technologies. Hydropower (traditional and low-impact) tends to sit near or modestly below parity, but patterns can vary by sheet/aggregation.
  • Interpretation & implications
    • The pattern suggests pipeline segmentation: BIPOC workers are more present in installation/efficiency/front-of-meter growth roles (e.g., Solar, EE), but are under-represented in legacy, higher-wage, or licensing-intensive technologies (e.g., Nuclear, Natural Gas).
    • Where the gap is negative and large, the sector likely faces barriers to entry and advancement (credentialing, prior experience screens, union access pathways, or geographic clustering). These are priority areas for equity interventions (e.g., apprenticeships, credential bridging, targeted recruitment, and sponsorship/mentorship).
